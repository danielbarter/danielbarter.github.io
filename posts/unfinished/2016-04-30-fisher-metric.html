The Fisher Metric

The Cramer-Rao Bound is a fundamental result in parametric inference. In this post, we are going to begin our journey towards a proof which uses tools from differential geometry.

Let \(X\) be a random variable and \(f(x,\theta)\) a family of possible distrubutions for \(X\), parameterized by \(\theta \in \Theta\), where \(\Theta\) is a manifold. The Fisher information metric is defined to be \[g = {\bf E}[(d_{\theta} \log f(X,\theta))^2].\] The map \(\theta \mapsto \log f(X,\theta)\) is a random function on \(\Theta\) and \(d_{\theta} \log f(X,\theta)\) is a random 1-form on \(\Theta\). The square \((d_{\theta} \log f(X,\theta))^2\) is a random symmetric 2-cotensor on \(\Theta\). Formally, \((d_{\theta} \log f(X,\theta))^2\) has rank 1, but recall that expectation satisfies \({\bf E}(XY) = {\rm Cov}(X,Y) + {\bf E}(X) {\bf E}(Y)\). This implies that the expectation of a tensor product of two non indipendent 1-tensors has rank greater than \(1\). In most cases of interest, \(g\) is non degenerate. Clearly, \(g\) is positive definite, therefore \(g\) is a Riemannian metric on the parameter manifold \(\Theta\).
